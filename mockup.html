A $50 donation in Montgomery County is worth $400 to a candidate. Here's the math.

The county's small-donor matching program turns a $50 check from a constituent into $400 of campaign money — but only for candidates who agree to forgo PAC dollars and stay under tight donation caps. The mechanics aren't obvious. They are also the entire point.

By Jane Reporter April 25, 2026 Capital News Service · 6 min read

When Marc Elrich first ran for Montgomery County Executive in 2018, he turned down nearly every donor who could give more than $150 and won anyway. He was one of the first three Maryland candidates to ever fund a county-level race entirely through small individual donations, and the program that made it possible — Bill 16-14, signed in 2014 — has since been imitated by Howard, Baltimore, and Prince George's counties.

The program works on a simple but unintuitive premise: the smaller the donation, the more the county is willing to match it. The first $50 a constituent gives a publicly-financed candidate is matched seven-to-one. The next $50 is matched four-to-one. The $50 after that, two-to-one. Everything past $150 from a single donor receives no match at all.

The result is a steep ramp at the bottom of the donation curve and a flat line above it — by design.

"It changes who you spend your time on the phone with. You stop calling the donor who can write you a $4,000 check, and you start calling fifty people who can each write you $50." — Marc Elrich, County Executive (2018-2026)
Tier 1 · The first $50

Why $50 of donor money becomes $400 of candidate money

One donor. One $50 check. The candidate has already qualified for matching by raising $40,000 from at least 500 in-jurisdiction residents.

$50 + $350 = $400 Donor money plus county match equals what the campaign actually spends.

The county's match is seven times the donor's contribution because this is Tier 1 — the highest match multiplier the program offers. Above $150, that multiplier drops to zero.

The next two tiers each add another $50 of donor money — but the county adds proportionally less each time. By the time a donor has given $150, the candidate has $800 to spend. Past $150, the donor can keep writing checks (up to a $500 cap), but the county stops matching.

Since 2014, every successful Montgomery County Executive — Elrich in 2018 and 2022, and Marc Elrich's successor in 2026 — has been publicly financed. So have a majority of the council. Whether the program will survive a court challenge from outside the county is a different question.
